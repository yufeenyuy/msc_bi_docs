**IT Compliance and IT Governance**

The central point of IT Governance is *Risks and Control*. Risk and Control is generally handled in six steps. These include:
1. Determining the risk level the organization is willing to take.
2. Perform *risk assessment* to identify threats or weaknesses that can harm the organization. Risk assessment is an activity undertaken to systematically examine and test an organization's risk and vulnerability level.
3. Rank identified threats according to the level of damage it can cause to the organization. This could be via quantitative(score ranging from 1 to 10) or qualitative(low, medium, high) measures.
4. Design risk mitigation, avoidance, acceptance or transfer solution
5 Implement designed solution while taking cost into consideration. This is where threats are being handled.
6. Document mitigation, avoidance, acceptance or transfer approach for future use in similar situations. 

In IT Governance, Risk is defined as the *effect of uncertainty on objectives*. But according to **NIST**,
risk is a *measure of the extent to which an entity is threatened by a potential circumstance or event, and typically a function of:*
- The adverse impacts that would arise if the circumstance of event occurs
- Likelihood of occurrence

Risk = Threat * Vulnerability * AsseValue

**Controls**

Risk assessment is an examination of control while controls are used to mitigate risks. A control is a tool which measures how an activity or transaction is performed and carried out in compliance with *laws, regulations, internal policies, and best practices*. According to NIST and ISO, a control is a measure that changes or affecs risk in a way that risk could be decreased, have no effect or in the worst case, may increase risk. There are three control categories:
1. Admistrative
2. Physical
3. Technical/logical

There are six control types:
1. Preventative: Controls designed to stop or prevent circumstances or events from occuring. Updating/Patching systems is an example of a preventive control action.
2. Deterrent: Controls that aim to dissuade or discourage unintented actions which threaten an organization.
3. Detective: Controls designed to identify and reveal circumstances or events that have occured or are yet to occure. Logs and monitoring tools are examples of detective controls.
4. Compensatory: Substitutes for controls that are implemented because of the challenge in implementing the ideal controls for the situation. E.g Using one license by several person because of lack of funds.
5. Directive: These controls attempt to force an outcome through mandatory or required actions. Examplae: COSO's "tone at the top" control environment for senior executives.
6. Corrective: Restore a process, repair a damange or stop damage from further escalating. They work together with detective controls.

**Control Shortcomings**

Controls do not inherently eliminate risk, actions or events. This is because they are subject to factors that make them vulnurable. The drawbacks of controls include:

+ Human erros which arise from unintentional mistakes.
+ Fraudulent activities which are made to to fulfil personal or joint endeavors or as a result of opportunities derived from shared password, unguarded valuables or unlocked cash drawers.
+ collusion, which is an agreement among two or more individuals to commit fraud. Also called conspiracy.
+ Management may use their position to override or cancel effective control systems. This may be for persoanl or legitimate reasons. For instance, a manager may bypass rules to create transactions.
+ Access creed, which is a situation where an employee can still actively use their credentials from a previous role at the same or different company to access systems are possible perform fraudulent activities. This can lead to undesired *segregation of duties*, as the right of legitimate person for the role may be compromised. Segregation of duties is a procedure that seeks reduce collusion by spliting responsibilities between two or more employees.

**Inherent Risk and Residual Risk**

+ Residual Risk: These are risk that remain after risks assessment and the implementation of risks controls.
+ Inherent Risk: Risk that exist in the absence of controls or when controls have been designed and implemented.

There are several approaches used by organizations to deal with risks. In general, it is done via one or more of the following actions:

1. Risk transfer: It doesn't mean that an organization becomes nonchalant, however, an organization transfers their risks to another entity. For instance, via insurance. In the case of data breaches or leakages, the insurance covers the cost entirely or partly.
2. Risk acceptance: Identified residual risk may be accepted by managers to avoid further expenses to handle this risk. In the event of damage as the result of accepted risk, the manager(s) who accepted the risk may be fired or reprimanded.
3. Risk avoidance: An organization may avoid purchasing or upgrading tools/services and continue running operations with existing tools that may no longer be actively maintained.

### ISO 31000:2018

This standard is made up of 3 main risk management sections which are, *principles, framework and process*. It provides a set of best practices and guideliness, instead of mandatory requirements. First created in 2009 and last released was in 2018. This standard provides 8 principles which revolve around *value creation and protection*.

*ISO 31000:2018 Risk Principles* 

+ Integrated: Risk management should be embeded at all levels of the organization from code reviews in software development to background check & credential/education verification in human resource.
+ Structured and comprehensive: A formalized risk management structure is recommended for consistent outcomes.
+ Customized: The risk management system must be customized to fit the input and outcomes of the organization.
+ Inclusive: All stakeholders must be involved in the formalized risk managment process. This is supported by the IT service management standards/frameworks like ITILV4 or ISO-1:20000.
+ Dynamic: Risk management program must adapt to new risks as they are evolving. E.g handling deep fakes.
+ Best available information: Quality(current and comprehensive) of information relating to risk is importanct.
+ Human and cultural factors: Human factors include management override, mistakes and other weaknesses and strengths. Culturally generally refers to the organization culture(siloed, flat, open command hierarchy, etc).
+ Continual improvement: Continuous improvement over time is a specific goal. This principle is common to other frameworks likt *ITIL and Six Sigma*.

*ISO 31000:2018 Risk Management Process*

This standard provides six activities that should be incorporated into an organization's policies, procedures, and practices. These activities are contained in section six of the standard and described below:

**6.2 communication and Consultation**: The organization's risk should be communicated to stakeholders especially if it concerns management's decision process. Consulation on the other hand is the involvement and participation of stakeholders and other experts parties in risk-related decision-making, with the aim to include all experts, and assign roles and responsibilities.
**6.3 Scope, context, and criteria**: Risk management is tailored to the unique goals, desired outcomes and relevant risk areas of the organization. This process is further broken into small subprocess described as follows:
    - **6.3.1 Defining Scope**: Questions should be asked to define the scope of operation. E.g Are ther laws and regulations that require certain elements within our risk management program?
    - **6.3.2* External and Internal context**: The risk management program should align with the internal and external operational environment of the organization.
    - **6.3.3 Defining risk criteria**: The risk criteria are fairly unique to each organization as it takes into account the industry type, competitors, regulators/regulatory burden, threat surface, corporate culture, goals, outcomes, stakeholders, management style and geographic locations. The risk criteria should change as the organization changes. It should incorporate directions for how risk criteria is measured/scored and reviewed.
**6.4 Risk assessment**: This is composed of risk identification, risk analysis and risk evaluation.
    - **6.4.1 Risk identification**: Ask questions that will simplify the identification of risk. E.g what is the organization doing to capture risk, and how are they identified?. What controls are in place to minimize the event of not capturing risk?. Where is risk coming from?. Where in the organization do threats and vulnerabilities lurk?
    - **6.4.2 Risk analysis**: What is the level of risk? Consider the likelihood and impact of negative outcomes and how effective the current inventory of controls is.
    - **6.4.3 Risk evaluation**: This is the result made after obtaining information from risk identification and risk analysis. Potential decision could be: do nothing or conduct further test to obtain more information about risk.
**6.5 Risk treatment**: Steps taken to address risk. This involves the following:
    - **6.5.1 Selection of risk treatment options**: Perform a cost benefit analysis on treatment options. Potential treatment could be: risk acceptance, risk transfer, risk avoidance and risk sharing.
    - **6.5.2 Preparing and implementing risk treatment plans**: The details for the selection of a treatment plan are documented formally including information regarding the selection that was made, who made the selection, were there alternatives, and when the treatment will take place and its costs.
**6.6 Monitoring and review**: The performance of the risk management program as well as its results are evaluated. Formal reviews and remidiation of shortcomings are documented and communicated to all relevant stakeholders.
**6.7 Recording and reporting**: This activity involves the communication of risk events, when a set threshhold is reached or exceeded, to internal and external stakeholders. This can be done at regular time intervals.

#### IT Security Frameworks: SOC 2, NIST 800 Series, ISO 27001:2013

**What is SOC 2?**

This is part of the American Institute of Certified Public Accountant's (AICPA) SSAE 18 Attestation standard. It is maintained and updated by AICPA. The COSO component of *risk managament and anti-fraud measures* have been incorporated into the latest release of the SOC 2. In five criteria, SOC 2 leverages a framework of controls used to tests the design and effectiveness of implemented controls responsible for ensuring security in IT systems and controling the processing of client/customer data. These criteria are: **Security, Privacy, Processing integrity, availability, annd confidentiality**. SOC 2 reports are being sold as the tools of choice for vendors. This is with the aim to show case the commitment of SOC 2 to *risk management and system security*. SOC 2 reports are only signed or attested by U.S Certified Public Accountants (CPA) and have a validity period of one year.

**What is NIST 800-30, 800-53?**

NIST 800-53 is an *IT security framework* developed by the U.S National Institute for Standard and Technology(U.S NIST). Initially, it was used to test IT Security Controls for US government entities and their vendors or contractors. Today, it has been widely adopted by several other companies. On the other hand, NIST 800-30 is a *Risk Management framework*. In general the National Institute for Standard and Technology (NIST) has created several frameworks, including 800-53 and 800-30 in its 800 series of IT frameworks.

**What is ISO/IEC 2 27001:2013?**

ISO/IEC 27001:2018 in other words ISO 27001 is the ISO/IEC Information Security Management System(ISMS) Standard. This framework contains 14 groups with 35 control categories, along with 114 individual controls and a *minimum of 15 mandatory written documents* which need to exist and be *fit for purpose*. An ISO 27000 Information Security Assessment Report can *only* be signed by an ISO-27000 certified individual(ISO Lead Auditor) working at a certified body(ISO certified audit firm). When an ISO 27001 Lead Auditor successfully tests, reviews and documents the state of the IT Management System, following the ISO 27001 requirements, the organization becomes ISO 27001 certified for a period of three years. During this period, the Lead Auditor will visit the firm to confirm that the organisation is using the ITMS program.

The phases of the action-oriented IT compliance process are discussed below:

1. Requirement Analysis Phase

The key points here are:
- Consideration of the regulation
- Involvement of in-house experts
- Measure definition and documentation

Because of the ambiguity or overlappings that exists in regulations stated by law or regulatory bodies, companies need an in-house expert in IT compliance topics. This expert is responsible for interpreting regulations and guiding the implementation of those required by the company. Before implement IT compliance regulation, the company must conduct an IT compliance requirement analysis. This can be done in of two approaches, namely:

+ Regulatory and legal perspective
+ Requirements of the company's various stakeholder groups.

It is recommended to follow the stakeholder groups approach. This is because different stakeholders understand the various needs of an organisation. As such they will precisely define the requirements of an organisation that need to be IT compliant. Additionally, it is also recommended to include stakeholder when defining a formal IT governance program for an organisation. By adopting this approach, an organisation will avoid fines, regulatory penalties and reputational damage. We saw that there exist frameworks(e.g COBIT) used in the implementation of IT governance programs. This also applias to IT compliance. The advantage of adopting IT compliance frameworks is that:

- It reduces implementation effort
- It covers many legal requirements
- It includes trial-and-testing measures.

Example frameworks or reference models that can be used to facilitate IT-compliance include:

* COBIT Framework
* IT-Grundschutz Katalog published by Bundesamt für Sicherheit in der Informationtechnik(BSI) or German Federal Office for Information Security.
